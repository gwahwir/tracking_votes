# Johor Political Landscape — Overview

## Introduction

Johor is Malaysia's southernmost state, sharing a 1km land border with Singapore across the Straits of Johor. With a population of approximately 4 million, it is one of Malaysia's most economically significant states due to its proximity to Singapore and the Iskandar Malaysia development corridor. Politically, Johor has been a pivotal battleground — historically an UMNO/BN fortress, transformed by GE14's "tsunami," partially restored to BN in the 2022 state election, and now contested among three coalitions. [Source: Wikipedia – Johor, 2024]

---

## Demographic Composition

Johor's electorate is multiracial, which distinguishes it from PAS's East Coast heartlands and makes it a genuine three-coalition battleground:

| Ethnic Group | Approx. % of Population |
|-------------|------------------------|
| Malay/Bumiputera | ~57% |
| Chinese | ~35% |
| Indian | ~6% |
| Others | ~2% |

This demographic split means no single coalition can win Johor on one community's votes alone. BN's 2022 state election dominance was achieved through strong Malay consolidation; DAP's urban Chinese base provides a reliable floor; PKR and Amanah compete for reform-minded Malay voters in the middle. ## Electoral Geography: Three Zones

**Zone 1 — Urban Johor Bahru and Iskandar corridor** (JB city, Skudai, Iskandar Puteri, Kulai)
- Dominant parties: DAP (Chinese urban), PKR (mixed), BN/MCA (Chinese community)
- High population density; large cross-border workforce; economically dynamic
- Highly sensitive to Singapore-Malaysia bilateral relations, housing costs, transport

**Zone 2 — Semi-urban towns** (Batu Pahat, Muar, Kluang, Segamat, Kota Tinggi)
- Mixed Malay-Chinese constituencies; classic swing zones
- BN competitive; DAP has seats; PKR contests
- Historically more conservative than JB; slower Iskandar growth spillover

**Zone 3 — Rural northern and eastern Johor** (Mersing, Pontian, Endau-Rompin)
- Predominantly Malay Muslim; agricultural and fishing communities
- BN/UMNO strongholds; growing PN/PAS competition
- Lower urbanisation; more sensitive to racial and religious identity politics

## Three-Coalition Dynamics (post-GE15)

Johor is contested by three coalitions:

| Coalition | Anchor Party | Johor Vote Base | Typical Johor Seats |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Barisan Nasional (BN)** | UMNO | Malay majority + BN Chinese/Indian | Dominant (40/56 DUN in 2022) |
| **Pakatan Harapan (PH)** | DAP + PKR + Amanah | Urban Chinese, reform-minded Malay, Indian | 12/56 DUN in 2022 |
| **Perikatan Nasional (PN)** | Bersatu + PAS | Rural Malay conservative | 3/56 DUN in 2022 |

Three-cornered contests (BN vs PH vs PN) are the default in most Johor constituencies. When Malay votes split between BN and PN, PH benefits; when Chinese votes split (MCA vs DAP), BN benefits. The mathematics of Johor elections therefore often hinge on **which Malay coalition consolidates the rural Malay vote**.

## The Johor Sultanate Factor

The Johor royal family (House of Temenggong) maintains a uniquely assertive political voice compared to other Malaysian royal houses. The **Sultan of Johor, Sultan Ibrahim Iskandar**, who became Malaysia's 17th Yang di-Pertuan Agong (King) in January 2024, has historically commented publicly on development, governance, and bilateral issues with Singapore. The Johorean political culture reflects a strong identification with the state's distinct history and royal legacy, which candidates and parties must navigate carefully. [Source: Wikipedia – Sultan of Johor, 2024]

## The Singapore Proximity Effect

Johor's political economy is uniquely shaped by its border with Singapore:
- **~350,000 people** cross the Johor-Singapore Causeway daily [Source: Wikipedia – Johor–Singapore Causeway, 2024]
- Estimated **100,000–150,000 Johor residents** work in Singapore, earning Singapore dollars
- This cross-border workforce is highly sensitive to SGD/MYR exchange rates, Causeway traffic, bilateral visa policies, and RTS Link progress
- Property prices in JB have been driven upward by Singapore-dollar purchasing power, creating affordability pressures for local Malay and Indian communities
- The **RTS Link** (opening January 2027) is a major election issue — seen as transformative infrastructure or, by critics, as deepening Singapore's economic influence over Johor

---

## Iskandar Malaysia Impact on Politics

The Iskandar Malaysia economic corridor (established 2006, spanning 2,300 km²) has reshaped Johor's electoral politics:
- Created a large **new middle class** of professionals in Iskandar Puteri, Nusajaya, and JB — relatively more reform-minded and DAP/PKR-leaning
- Brought in large numbers of **foreign workers and expatriates**, raising demographic concerns among some Malay voters
- Land acquisition for development displaced some **Orang Asal and rural Malay communities**, creating anti-development grievances that PN/PAS exploit
- The **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)**, announced 2024, deepens this trend further [STALE — last confirmed: 2024]

## Historical Arc: From Fortress to Battleground

| Election | Key Outcome |
|----------|-------------|
| Pre-2008 | UMNO/BN dominance; Johor considered "safe state" |
| GE12 (2008) | BN majority reduced; DAP gains in urban JB |
| GE13 (2013) | BN holds Johor but opposition grows in urban seats |
| **GE14 (2018)** | **PH wins Johor state government**; "political tsunami" sweeps Malaysia |
| 2020 | Sheraton Move collapses PH government nationally; Johor enters political uncertainty |
| **Johor SE 2022** | **BN wins 40/56 DUN seats**; regains dominance under Hasni Mohammad |
| **GE15 (2022)** | Three-way contest; BN wins most Johor Parlimen seats but PN performs better than 2018 |

[Source: Wikipedia – 2022 Johor state election, 2024; Wikipedia – 2022 Malaysian general election, 2024]

## Key Electoral Themes (Current Cycle)

1. **Cost of living (kos sara hidup)** — dominant issue across all demographics; JB property, food, petrol costs
2. **Johor-Singapore bilateral relations** — RTS Link, Causeway, JS-SEZ negotiations, water agreement renewal
3. **Malay unity vs. multiracial coalition** — BN and PN compete to frame who best defends Malay interests
4. **Development equity** — who benefits from Iskandar Malaysia growth? Displacement of lower-income communities
5. **Corruption and governance** — BN's 1MDB legacy; Muhyiddin's legal cases; institutional reform demands
6. **Youth voters (Undi18)** — expanded electorate since GE15; uncertain loyalty; more urban, digital-native
