# Johor Bahru – Singapore Relations

## Overview

Johor Bahru (JB) and Singapore share a 1.056km land border across the Straits of Johor, connected by the **Johor-Singapore Causeway**. The bilateral relationship is uniquely intensive: hundreds of thousands of people cross daily, a significant portion of Johor's working population earns in Singapore dollars, and the two governments are jointly developing the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)**. Understanding this relationship is essential for interpreting Johor electoral politics.

---

## The Johor-Singapore Causeway

**Physical specifications:**
- Length: 1.056 km
- Carries: motor vehicles, bicycles, pedestrian walkway, and the KTM Shuttle Tebrau rail service
- Built: 1923; the primary land crossing since independence

**Traffic volume:**
- ~350,000 daily travellers (typical range: 250,000–400,000)
- **Peak traffic: 510,000 crossings** on 28 March 2024 — the highest ever recorded at Singapore's land borders
- The Causeway is one of the world's busiest land border crossings

**Dual function:**
- Transportation corridor for commuters, goods, and tourism
- Carries a water pipeline supplying Singapore under the 1962 Water Agreement

[Source: Wikipedia – Johor–Singapore Causeway, 2024]

## The Cross-Border Workforce

**Scale:** Approximately **350,000 Johor residents** cross into Singapore for work regularly, earning Singapore dollars (SGD). With the SGD/MYR exchange rate typically around 1 SGD = 3.2–3.5 MYR, this creates a significant income differential that shapes:

- **Property demand in JB** — cross-border earners can afford higher JB property prices; this prices out local ringgit earners
- **Retail and food prices** — JB's proximity to Singapore inflates costs for non-commuters
- **Political sensitivity to bilateral relations** — any disruption to Singapore-Malaysia relations (visas, border rules, toll charges) is immediately felt by this large voter bloc

**Electoral significance:** The cross-border workforce is a powerful voting bloc in JB urban constituencies. Parties that credibly promise smooth bilateral relations and improved Causeway/RTS infrastructure tend to attract these votes. DAP and PKR have historically been seen as more Singapore-friendly; BN's official bilateral relationship management also matters.

## The RTS Link (Johor Bahru–Singapore Rapid Transit System)

**What it is:** A 4km elevated/underground rail link connecting **Bukit Chagar station (Johor Bahru)** to **Woodlands North station (Singapore)**. It will allow commuters to travel between JB and Singapore in approximately **5 minutes**.

**Timeline:**
- Announced: 2010 (bilateral agreement)
- Construction: Multiple delays and restarts due to bilateral negotiations
- Current status (as of April 2026): **~90% construction completion**
- Projected completion: December 2026
- Operations launch: **January 2027**

**Capacity:**
- 8 x four-car trains; 607 passengers per train (normal capacity)
- Initial daily ridership: ~40,000 passengers
- Long-term capacity: ~140,000 daily passengers
- Peak headway: 3.6 minutes

**Fares:** RM15.50–RM21.70 (approximately SGD 5–7) per trip [as of February 2026]

**Traffic relief:** Expected to reduce Causeway traffic by at least **35%**

[Source: Wikipedia – Johor Bahru–Singapore Rapid Transit System, 2024]

### RTS Link as an Election Issue

The RTS Link has been a recurring election issue in Johor Bahru constituencies for over a decade due to repeated delays:
- **Pro-RTS voters** (urban commuters, professionals) see it as transformative infrastructure that will cut daily 2–3 hour Causeway commutes to 5 minutes
- **Critics** raise concerns about: land use around Bukit Chagar, developer profits linked to political connections, whether Singapore benefits more than Johor
- **PN/PAS framing** — some voices raise concerns about Singapore's growing economic influence over Johor; RTS Link symbolises deeper integration, which can be framed negatively in nationalist rhetoric
- The January 2027 opening date makes RTS Link a live election issue in any near-term Johor election

## The Water Agreement

Under the **1962 Water Agreement**, Johor supplies Singapore with raw water at a fixed price. The agreement expires in **2061** and periodic renegotiations — particularly over the price of water — have been a source of bilateral tension. Key facts:
- Singapore pays Johor a price of 3 Malaysian cents per 1,000 gallons of raw water (set in 1962; widely considered below market rate)
- Johor buys back treated water from Singapore at a higher price
- Water negotiations have historically been politically charged in Malaysia; UMNO/BN has used them to demonstrate negotiating toughness with Singapore
- Any news about water negotiations is electorally sensitive in Johor

## Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)

In January 2024, Malaysia and Singapore officially launched the **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)**, a joint initiative to develop a high-value economic zone in Johor Bahru and Iskandar Puteri with preferential policies for investors. Key features:
- Simplified customs and immigration procedures for registered workers and companies
- Tax incentives for high-value industries: semiconductors, aerospace MRO, medical devices, data centres
- Joint Malaysia-Singapore committee for governance

**Electoral implications:**
- JS-SEZ is seen by BN and PH state government as a major economic win for Johor
- PN/PAS have raised concerns about Singapore's disproportionate influence and the impact on Malay workers and landowners
- The JS-SEZ has accelerated property investment in JB, intensifying the affordability debate [STALE — last confirmed: 2024]

## Tone Guidance for Analysis

Articles about JB-Singapore relations should be analysed with awareness that:
1. **Malaysian nationalist framing** — any suggestion Singapore benefits more than Malaysia can be amplified politically
2. **Royal family sensitivity** — the Sultan of Johor (now Malaysia's King) has personal business and diplomatic relationships with Singapore; direct criticism of bilateral deals can implicate royal interests
3. **POFMA / contempt risks** — Malaysian and Singaporean defamation and fake news laws create a chilling effect on some reporting about bilateral arrangements
4. **Economic anxiety vs. optimism** — the same bilateral integration news can be framed as prosperity by PH/BN or as a sovereignty threat by PN/PAS
