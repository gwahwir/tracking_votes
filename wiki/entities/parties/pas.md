# Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS) — Malaysian Islamic Party

## Overview

**Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS)** — Malaysian Islamic Party — is Malaysia's principal Islamist party, founded on 24 November 1951 as the Pan Malayan Islamic Union (legalised 31 May 1955 as the Pan-Malayan Islamic Party). It split from UMNO under Ahmad Fuad Hassan over UMNO's perceived insufficient commitment to Islam. [Source: Wikipedia – Malaysian Islamic Party, 2024]

PAS is one of the two anchor parties of the **Perikatan Nasional (PN)** coalition alongside Bersatu. As of GE15 (2022), PAS is the **single largest party by parliamentary seat count** in Malaysia with 43 seats.

---

## Ideology

- **Islamism** — establishment of an Islamic state governed by Syariah law
- Long-term goal: full implementation of **Hudud** (Islamic criminal law) across Malaysia
- **Malay dominance** — closely aligned with Malay ethnic interests
- Social conservatism: opposes gambling, alcohol, mixed-gender events, LGBTQ+ rights
- Internationally affiliated with the **Muslim Brotherhood**

## Geographic Strongholds

PAS's primary dominance is in the **East Coast states**:
- **Kelantan** — governed by PAS since 1990 (longest consecutive state rule in Malaysia)
- **Terengganu** — governed by PAS/PN
- **Perlis** — PAS/PN coalition
- **Kedah** — PAS/PN coalition

These are Malaysia's most rural, Malay-Muslim-majority states. [Source: Wikipedia – Malaysian Islamic Party, 2024]

## Johor Presence

PAS has historically had limited presence in Johor, which is more urbanised and multiracial than PAS's East Coast heartlands. However, PAS's GE15 surge reflected growing Malay rural conservative sentiment across Malaysia, including in Johor's northern and eastern districts.

**2022 Johor State Election:**
- PAS won **1 of 56 DUN seats** as part of PN
- PAS contested in predominantly Malay rural constituencies in northern Johor (Segamat, Kluang, Mersing districts)

**Key observation:** PAS's Johor vote share has grown in each election since 2018, reflecting the nationwide "green wave" (ombak hijau) of Malay religious conservatism. In GE15, PN/PAS performed strongly even in Johor constituencies where they did not win, suggesting growing potential. [STALE — last confirmed: 2024]

## Key Issues in Johor Context

- **Islamic state agenda** — PAS's Hudud push is a galvanising issue for both supporters (conservative rural Malay voters) and opponents (non-Muslim voters who see it as a threat to constitutional secularism)
- **"Green wave" narrative** — PAS frames GE15 as evidence that Malay Muslims are rejecting the "liberal" PH agenda; this narrative is amplified in Johor by PN campaign materials
- **Anti-DAP positioning** — PAS's most effective Johor messaging frames any vote for PH as a vote for DAP's "Chinese agenda"; highly effective in rural Malay Johor constituencies
- **Moral governance** — PAS-governed states are held up as examples of clean, corruption-free administration (though critics dispute this); this resonates in Johor where BN's corruption legacy is well-known
- **Iskandar Malaysia concerns** — some PAS voices raise concerns about the social impact of Singapore-adjacent development (casino rumours, foreign workers, cultural dilution)

---

## The "Green Wave" in Johor

The 2022 GE15 saw a significant "green wave" (ombak hijau) — a surge in Malay voter support for PN/PAS. While this was most dramatic in Peninsular Malaysia's rural states, it also manifested in Johor:
- Several BN-held Johor Parlimen seats saw reduced majorities due to three-cornered contests splitting the Malay vote between BN and PN
- In some DUN constituencies, PN finished second, suggesting a growing base for future elections
- Bridget Welsh's analysis of the 2022 Johor state election noted that while BN won 40/56 seats, opposition parties collectively won the popular vote in 35/56 constituencies — suggesting BN's margin masks underlying voter fragmentation [Source: Bridget Welsh, bridgetwelsh.com, 2022]

---

## Voter Base Profile

- **Rural Malay Muslim voters** in northern Johor (Segamat, Mersing, Kluang hinterlands)
- **Religious conservatives** opposed to the liberalising influence of JB urbanisation
- **Younger Malay voters** radicalised through social media Islamic content (TikTok, Instagram)
- PAS is weakest in urban Johor (JB city, Iskandar Puteri, Kulai) where multiracial demographics dilute its appeal
